## Summary — *Phishing Activity Trends Report, 4th Quarter 2025*

This APWG report presents phishing trends observed between **October and December 2025**. It is not an academic paper, but a technical industry report with recent data about phishing attacks, social media threats, QR code phishing and Business Email Compromise. 

## Main objective

The report aims to measure how phishing and identity theft techniques evolved during Q4 2025. APWG collects data from member companies, research partners, reports submitted through its website, emails sent to `reportphishing@apwg.org`, and phishing URLs submitted to the APWG eCrime eXchange. 

## Main numbers from Q4 2025

In the fourth quarter of 2025, APWG observed **853,244 phishing attacks**, which was a **4% decrease** compared with Q3 2025. Across the whole year, APWG observed **3.8 million phishing attacks**, slightly above the **3.76 million** reported in 2024. 

The monthly numbers were:

| Month    | Unique phishing websites / attacks | Unique phishing email campaigns | Brands targeted |
| -------- | ---------------------------------: | ------------------------------: | --------------: |
| October  |                            269,558 |                          16,284 |             457 |
| November |                            287,995 |                          14,980 |             461 |
| December |                            295,691 |                          14,091 |             496 |

The chart on page 4 shows that phishing remained consistently high throughout 2025, with a peak around Q2 and a slight decrease toward Q4.

The pie chart on page 5 shows that **Social Media** and **SaaS/Webmail** were the most targeted categories, while attacks against telecom providers increased significantly compared with the previous quarter. 

## Rise of SMS phishing

The report notes that **smishing**, or SMS-based phishing, continued to grow. Crane Authentication observed steady **30% to 40% quarter-over-quarter growth** in SMS-based fraud detections. This is important because SMS allows attackers to bypass traditional email filters and reach victims directly on their phones. 

For your topic, this supports the idea that modern phishing is no longer limited to email. Security filters must now consider several communication channels: email, SMS, social media, QR codes and messaging platforms.

## Social media threats

ZeroFox found that threats on social media in 2025 were mainly divided into two categories:

| Threat type           | Share |
| --------------------- | ----: |
| Scams                 |   52% |
| Impersonation         |   34% |
| Unauthorized logo use |   10% |
| Claimed relationship  |    2% |
| Piracy                |    1% |
| Other                 |    1% |

The chart on page 6 shows that **scams and impersonation together represented 86% of confirmed social media threats**. This is relevant because phishing increasingly uses fake profiles, fake brands, fake ads and impersonation instead of only traditional malicious emails. 

The report also states that threat volume increased on every major social media platform during 2025, with quarter-to-quarter growth ranging from **51% to 843%**. 

## QR code phishing

The report gives special attention to QR code attacks, also called **quishing**. These attacks place malicious QR codes in emails or attachments. When scanned, the QR code sends the victim to a phishing website or malware download page. Traditional email filters often struggle with this because the malicious URL is hidden inside an image. 

In Q4 2025, Mimecast detected **655,673 unique malicious QR codes**, a **9% decrease** from Q3 2025. However, the numbers remain very high. The report also notes that attackers timed QR phishing around **Black Friday**, when users are more likely to trust order, delivery and payment notifications. 

Attackers abuse QR code platforms because some allow:

* free QR creation;
* tracking of scans;
* changing the destination URL after generation;
* redirecting through URL shorteners;
* hiding the final malicious destination.

The chart on page 10 shows that QR phishing targeted several sectors, with **Professional Services**, **Manufacturing** and **Finance** among the most affected. Walmart was the most impersonated brand in QR code attacks, representing **61%** of detected QR-code brand attacks. 

## Business Email Compromise

The report also analyses **Business Email Compromise**, known as **BEC**. In these attacks, criminals impersonate an employee, executive, vendor or trusted contact to trick someone into sending money or sensitive information. 

Fortra found that in Q4 2025:

* the average requested amount in wire transfer BEC attacks was **$50,297**;
* wire transfer BEC attacks increased by **136%** compared with Q3;
* gift card scams were the most common BEC cash-out method, representing **59%**;
* wire transfers represented **17%**;
* payroll diversion represented **8%**.

The pie chart on page 11 shows that gift cards remained the dominant cash-out method, but wire transfer attempts grew significantly. 

The report attributes much of the wire transfer growth to **Scripted Sparrow**, described as a prolific BEC group sending up to **6 million highly targeted emails monthly**, often targeting Accounts Payable teams with fake executive coaching invoices. 

## Key conclusions

The most important conclusions are:

1. **Phishing remains highly active**, with millions of attacks per year.
2. **Social media and SaaS/Webmail are major targets**.
3. **SMS phishing is growing**, showing that attackers are moving beyond email.
4. **QR code phishing is a serious challenge** because it can bypass traditional filters.
5. **BEC attacks are financially dangerous**, especially wire transfer and gift card scams.
6. **Attackers constantly shift infrastructure and brands**, making static blocklists insufficient.
7. **Modern phishing requires multi-channel detection**, not just email filtering.

## Relevance for your project

This report is very useful for your topic **“Modern Phishing vs. Security Filters”** because it gives recent evidence that phishing is evolving across channels.

You can use it to support arguments such as:

* traditional email filters are not enough;
* phishing now happens through email, SMS, social media, QR codes and impersonation;
* attackers use trusted brands, seasonal events and legitimate platforms to increase credibility;
* QR codes and URL shorteners make detection harder;
* modern filters must combine URL analysis, image/QR analysis, sender authentication, social media monitoring, behavioral detection and user education.

## Final takeaway

The main takeaway is that phishing in 2025 is no longer just suspicious emails with fake links. It has become a multi-channel ecosystem involving **social media scams, impersonation, SMS phishing, QR code phishing and BEC attacks**. For your article, this report is useful as recent statistical evidence that modern phishing is adapting faster than traditional security filters.
