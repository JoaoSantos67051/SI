# Lab #06

In my opinion, the article shows that cybersecurity is entering a new phase where traditional detection methods are no longer enough by themselves. Tools such as scanners, manual audits, and penetration tests are still important, but they have limits. If an AI system can find and exploit vulnerabilities that existed for many years, then the problem is not only that organizations are slow to patch, but also that some weaknesses are too complex for normal tools to detect easily.

I think this creates a major challenge for blue teams. Blue teams will need to move from a reactive model to a more continuous and intelligent defense model. It will not be enough to wait for alerts or scan systems occasionally. Defenders will need better visibility, faster patching, stronger monitoring, and AI-assisted tools that can help them understand attack paths before real attackers use them.

For red teams, this also changes the future of offensive security. AI can help red teams discover more complex vulnerabilities, simulate realistic attack chains, and test systems more deeply. However, this also means that red team work must become more responsible and controlled. If AI makes exploitation faster and easier, then authorization, scope, ethics, and reporting become even more important.

Overall, I believe the future of cybersecurity will depend on how well red teams and blue teams adapt to AI. Red teams will use AI to test systems more effectively, while blue teams will need AI to detect, prioritize, and respond faster. The main lesson from the article is that security teams cannot trust only traditional methods. They must assume that attackers will also use AI and prepare their defenses for a faster, more automated, and more complex threat landscape.
